Precession of the perihelion of Mercury

Find out one of the most famous predictions of General Relativity

June 10, 2023 Ronaldo Camacho

Brief Overview

The problem of Mercury perihelion was well known in Einstein's time. The issue was observed in the works of french astronomer Urbain Le Verrier (1859) when he calculated a perturbation effect caused by interaction of Mercury with other planets, finding an anomally: the perihelion advance. This problem could not be explained by Newton's theory and remaind that way until the arrival of General Relativity, becoming one of its most famous predictions.

In order to understand this problem and its eventual solution, let us start from the basics. It is well known that all planets orbit the Sun in ellipses, as described by Kepler's Laws, so it is only natural that one point in their orbit will be closest to the Sun and another point farthest from it. The farthest point is known as aphelion, while the closest one is the perihelion. As observed by Le Verrier, the perihelion advances by a small amount at each orbit of Mercury. Using Newton's theory scientists at the time were able to calculate this advance, but there was still an approximate 43 arc-second per century discrepancy between the Newtonian prediction and the observed precession.

It was only on November 18th of 1915 that Mercury's perihelion would be conquered. Shortly before arriving at the final field equations of General Relativity, Einstein published a derivation of Mercury's orbital precession based on the vacuum field equations, which would be unchanged in the final theory. It was an immense success not only for Physics but for Eintein himself. Since the publication of his Special Relativity in 1905, he had been working on a grander theory of gravitation. In fact, in 1907 he had written to Conrad Habicht telling about his work on a theory that he hoped would account for the anomalous precession of Mercury.

Orbit of a massive particle

Let's start by deriving the equation which control the orbit of a massive particle in the Schwarzschild geometry. In order to do that we will use the Killing vector associated with the time independence of the Schwarzschild metric, \(\xi^\alpha = (1,0,0,0)\), and the Killing vector associated with its angular independence, \(\eta^\alpha = (0,0,0,1)\). The reasoning behind this is the fact that, since the metric is independent of \(\,t\,\) and \(\varphi\), the quantities \(\xi \cdot u\) and \(\eta \cdot u\) are conserved, i.e.

\begin{equation} e = -\xi \cdot u = \left( 1-\frac{2GM}{r} \right)\,\frac{dt}{d\tau} = \hbox{constant}, \end{equation}

\begin{equation} l = -\eta \cdot u = r^2\sin^2\theta \frac{d\varphi}{d\tau} = r^2\frac{d\varphi}{d\tau} = \hbox{constant}. \end{equation}

From the condition \(u \cdot u = u^\alpha \, g_{\alpha \beta} \, u^\beta = -1\) we get

(If the equation is out of the screen rotate your phone for better visualization)

\begin{equation} -\left( \frac{e}{1-\frac{2GM}{r}} \right)^2 \left(1-\frac{2GM}{r}\right) + \left(\frac{dr}{d\tau}\right)^2\left(1-\frac{2GM}{r}\right)^{-1} + \frac{l^2}{r^4}r^2 = -1, \end{equation} where we used \begin{equation} u^0 = \frac{e}{1-2GMr^{-1}}, \quad u^1 = \frac{dr}{d\tau}, \quad u^2 = 0, \quad u^3 = \frac{l}{r^2}. \end{equation}

Working out the math we get \begin{equation} \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{dr}{d\tau}\right)^2 + \frac{1}{2}\left[\left(1+\frac{l^2}{r^2}\right)\left(1-\frac{2GM}{r}\right)-1\right] = \frac{e^2-1}{2} \end{equation} which can be simplyfied to \begin{equation} \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{dr}{d\tau}\right)^2 + V_{eff}(r) = E, \end{equation} where \begin{equation} V_{eff}(r) = \frac{1}{2}\left[\left(1+\frac{l^2}{r^2}\right)\left(1-\frac{2GM}{r}\right)-1\right] = -\frac{GM}{r}+\frac{l^2}{2r^2}-\frac{GMl^2}{r^3} \end{equation} is the effective potential energy and \begin{equation} E = \frac{e^2-1}{2} \end{equation} is the total energy. We can already see why this theory succeed where Newton's failed, i.e. comparing the equation we derived above to the orbit of a planet of mass \(m=1\) in newtonian gravity we will see that the mechanical energy is \begin{equation} E = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{dr}{dt}\right)^2 + \frac{l^2}{2r^2} - \frac{GM}{r}. \end{equation}

Therefore, we see that the relativistic approach has an additional \(-\frac{GMl^2}{r^3}\) term. For large values of \(r\) the potential is close to the Newtonian effective potential for motion in a \(1/r\) potential, due to the fact that both theories share the first two terms. However, as \(r\) decreases, the attractive \(1/r^3\) correction from General Relativity becomes increasingly important. This result is shown in Figure 1 bellow.

Finally, relating \(r\) with \(\varphi\) we can study the orbit, i.e. \begin{equation} \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{l}{r^2}\frac{dr}{d\varphi}\right)^2 + -\frac{GM}{r}+\frac{l^2}{2r^2}-\frac{GMl^2}{r^3} = E, \end{equation} which we can then parametrize with \(u \equiv r^{-1}\) in order to get \begin{equation} \frac{d^2u}{d\varphi^2} + u = \frac{GM}{l^2} + 3GMu^2. \end{equation} This is the equation we were looking for.

Nearly circular orbit

We could, in principle, solve this equation exactly. However, the algebra becomes rather complicated and it is beyond our intentions in this article. As an alternative we will solve it for a nearly circular orbit. In order to do that, consider first a circular orbit with radius \(r_c\) so that \(u = u_c = r_c^{-1}\). The quantity \(u_c\) satisfies our previous equation with a constant value \begin{equation} u_c = \frac{GM}{l^2} + 3GMu_c^2. \end{equation}

Now, let us consider the nearly circular orbit \(u(\varphi) = u_c[1+\omega(\varphi)]\), with \(\omega << 1\). Inserting this in our equation we get \begin{equation} u_c \frac{d^2\omega}{d\varphi^2} + u_c\omega + u_c = \frac{GM}{l^2} + 3GM\{u_c[1+\omega]\}^2, \end{equation} which can be simplyfied in order to obtain an equation for \(\omega\), i.e. \begin{equation} \frac{d^2\omega}{d\varphi^2} + \omega(1-6GMu_c) = 0. \end{equation}

In practice, what we did here was simply contruct a linear equation for the "perturbation \(\omega\)" which is a standard method for dealing with inconvenient differential equations. Now what we have is the equation for the simple harmonic oscilator which can be solved by \begin{equation} \omega = A\cos(\sqrt{1-6GMu_c}\varphi). \end{equation}

Going back to our nearly circular orbit we can see that \(u(\varphi) = u_c[1+A\cos(\sqrt{1-6GMu_c}\varphi)]\) or, in terms of \(r = u^{-1}\), \begin{equation} r(\varphi) = \frac{r_c}{1+A\cos\left(\sqrt{1-\frac{6GM}{r_c}}\varphi\right)}. \end{equation}

At this point we can take a moment to understand this result. It is possible to think about three scenarios:

  • Perihelion when the argument of cosine is 0 \(\Rightarrow r = \frac{r_c}{1+A}\)
  • Aphelion when the argument of cosine is \(\pi\) \(\Rightarrow r = \frac{r_c}{1-A}\)
  • New perihelion when the argument is \(2\pi\) \(\Rightarrow r = \frac{r_c}{1+A}\)
Thus, at each orbit the argument of the cosine changes by \(2\pi\), which translates to \begin{equation} 1 \; \hbox{orbit} \Leftrightarrow \sqrt{1-\frac{6GM}{r_c}}\Delta \varphi = 2\pi. \end{equation} In the weak field approximation \(GM << r\), therefore \begin{equation} \Delta \varphi = \frac{2\pi}{\sqrt{1-\frac{6GM}{r_c}}} \approx 2\pi + \frac{6\pi GM}{r_c}. \end{equation}

A simple way to interpret this result is using the concept of closed orbit. Starting from the perihelion, \(r(\tau)\) will grow until we get to aphelion and then decreases back to the perihelion. In the meantime, if \(\varphi\) has changed by \(2\pi\) we say that the orbit is closed, otherwise we define the amount of precession per orbit by \begin{equation} \delta \varphi_{prec} \equiv \Delta \varphi_{orbit} - 2\pi. \end{equation}

In our case, \begin{equation} \delta \varphi_{prec} = \Delta \varphi_{orbit} - 2\pi = \frac{6\pi GM}{r_c}. \end{equation} In the weak field regime, \(r_c = \frac{l^2}{GM}\), so we finally get the amount of precession predicted by General Relativity: \begin{equation} \delta \varphi_{prec} = 6\pi \left(\frac{GM}{l^2}\right). \end{equation}

Mercury's precession

We now have all the tools we need to evaluate the precession of Mercury. To do that we use our last equation and remember that the angular momentum for Mercury is \begin{equation} l = rv \approx 4.60 \cdot 10^7 \, km \cdot 59.0 \, km/s \approx 2.71 \cdot 10^5\, m^2/s. \end{equation}

Taking the mass of the Sun as \(M_\odot \approx 1.99 \cdot 10^{30}\) kg, we can then calculate the variation of the precession: \begin{equation} \delta \varphi_{prec} \approx 6\pi \left( \frac{6.67 \cdot 10^{-11} \frac{m^3}{kg\,s^2} 1.99 \cdot 10^{30}\,kg}{2.71 \cdot 10^{15} \frac{m^2}{s} 3 \cdot 10^8 \frac{m}{s}} \right)^2 \approx 5.02 \cdot 10^{-7}\,rad, \end{equation} a value that is accumulated for each orbit. Since Mercury has an orbital period around 88 days we can then calculate how much its orbit is affected in a century, i.e. \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\delta \varphi}{T} &\approx& \frac{5.02 \cdot 10^{-7}\,\hbox{rad}}{2.41 \cdot 10^{-3} \cdot 100 \,\hbox{yrs}} \approx 2.08 \cdot 10^{-4}\,\hbox{rad/100yrs}\\ \\ &=& \frac{2.08 \cdot 10^{-4} \, \frac{180}{\pi}\,\hbox{degrees}}{100\,\hbox{yrs}} = \frac{2.08 \cdot 10^{-4} \, \frac{180}{\pi}\,3600\,\hbox{seconds}}{100\,\hbox{yrs}}\\ \\ &\approx& 43"/100yrs \end{eqnarray}
